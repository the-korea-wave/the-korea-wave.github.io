On the afternoon of July 23, the highest-performing retail traders at Mirae Asset Securities — the group ranked in the top 1% by one-month return — moved decisively back into South Korea's two largest memory-chip makers. SK Hynix and Samsung Electronics topped their net-buying list, with Hyundai Motor rounding out the leaders, while the same cohort lightened up on Naver, SK Square and Samsung Heavy Industries. It marked a reunion of the "Samjeon-nix" pairing — a market nickname for holding Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix together — after several days of the two names pulling in opposite directions.

## A Week of Whiplash Between the Two Chipmakers

The July 23 alignment stands out precisely because the preceding sessions did not hold to it. On July 20, the top-percentile traders bought Samsung Electronics more heavily than anything else and sold SK Hynix throughout the day; Samsung Electro-Mechanics, a morning net-sell, flipped to the number-two buy by the afternoon. The pattern then inverted on July 21, when SK Hynix, SK Square and SK Telecom took the top three buying spots and Samsung Electronics was sold off. By July 22 the pendulum swung again, with Samsung Electronics reclaiming the top buy and SK Hynix landing back among the day's largest disposals.

That day-to-day reversal — accumulate one chipmaker, dump the other, then reverse the next session — underscores how tactically this cohort has been trading the memory complex rather than committing to a single winner. The July 23 move to hold both names at once is the first time in the stretch that the rivalry gave way to buying the pair together.

## Why These Two Names Anchor the Tape

SK Hynix and Samsung Electronics are not interchangeable bets so much as the two Korean pillars of a global memory oligopoly. Both sit among the three dominant producers of memory chips, a tier they share with Micron. SK Hynix, headquartered in Icheon and focused on DRAM and NAND flash, posted sales of roughly 66.19 trillion won in the 2024 fiscal year and employed 46,863 people — a scale that makes its daily direction a proxy for sentiment on the memory cycle itself.

Samsung Electronics carries even broader weight as the flagship of the country's largest family-controlled conglomerate. Samsung Group, founded in 1938 by Lee Byung-chul as a trading firm, remains the biggest chaebol in South Korea, and as of 2024 the brand ranked fifth worldwide by valuation. When traders rotate between the two, they are effectively expressing a view on how the memory upcycle is distributed across the sector's leaders.

## The Names They Sold, and What Rounds Out the Buy List

The July 23 disposals tell their own story. SK Square, a holding company whose portfolio is closely tied to the SK semiconductor franchise, was sold alongside SK Hynix's stablemate names on some days and bought on others — a sign the cohort is separating the operating chipmaker from its affiliated holding structure. Naver, the Seongnam-based internet platform that runs South Korea's leading search portal along with mapping, payments and its Line and CLOVA X products, was trimmed as capital rotated toward hardware. Samsung Heavy Industries, which ranked as the world's second-largest shipbuilder by 2010 revenue, also landed among the sells.

On the buy side, Hyundai Motor's presence broadens the trade beyond chips. The automaker, established in 1967 and holding a 33.88% stake in Kia, built 4,146,335 vehicles in 2024 and reported revenue of 186.3 trillion won for the 2025 fiscal year — a large, liquid industrial name that pairs naturally with memory as a bet on Korea's export engine.

## Reading the Signal Without Overreading It

These flows reflect one venue — Mirae Asset Securities, the largest South Korean brokerage by market capitalization — and one narrow, high-performing slice of its clients. That makes the data a sharp but partial lens: it captures how the most successful short-term traders are positioning, not the market's full breadth. The consistent takeaway across the week is less about any single session's winner than about the intensity of rotation within the Samsung–SK Hynix axis, and the July 23 decision to stop choosing and hold both.

For readers tracking the memory trade, the question the coming sessions will answer is whether this reunion of the pair marks a durable shift toward buying the complex as a whole, or simply another turn in a week that has rewarded traders for switching sides quickly.
